Introduction

    
    	The layout and structure of cities has a significant impact on the air quality inside the city 
        ("the built environment has a more direct impact on morning pollutant concentrations, it also appears 
        to influence afternoon concentrations" Liye Zhu. et al, (2021).), smog is a more visual example of how 
        urban areas influence air quality ("it is prevalent in cities all over the world" Fiona Green, (2025).). 
        Tall buildings (especially in series) diverge the air around them in a way that creates a circulating air 
        current on the back face ("it drives the air into the bottom of the model, forming a comparative weak 
        vortex" Dan Mei. el al, (2018).), this vortex is too weak to reach the streat from most tall buildings so 
        pollutants diffuse into the atmosphere slower and lingers up to 10m above ground, which could potentially 
        cause smog as it is more likely to appear with higher exposure to sunlight, ("Sunlight causes volatile 
        organic compounds to react with nitrogen oxides to form ground-level ozone" Fiona Green, (2025).).

Analysis and discussion

    
    Hight Arrangement
	Aside from the negative health impacts of still, polluted air in urban areas, it can also cause an 
	unpleasent and musty smell alongside lower visability. Hight and density of buildings is an important 
    	factor that contributes to the pollution residue at street level as winds struggle with reaching the 
    	bottom of buildings and spread greenhouse gasses away from the city. Figure 1 shows how arranging the 
        buildings in accending order from the direction of the wind could help increase the availability of 
        new air at lower hights, this is because more buildings are able to interfere with the wind, and as the 
        wind can be modeled as a fluid, the area arround the interference is effected less (but still noticeably), 
        therefore the other buildings would still experience airflow. 
        A level hight arrangement is able to have some airflow between the buildings due to the same phenomenon 
        as your car humming with open windows on the motorway, however this is barely effective due to the hight
        of the buildings. An even worse layout of buildings is in decending order facing the wind, this is 
        because the tallest building shields the others from the wind so no vortexes are able to be formed 
        and the air between the building would be mostly still.
        
    Layout Solution
        Using figure 3 we can see that a spacious layout allows for more airflow, seen by the lack of 
        lack of red areas in the diagram. Open areas and clear pathways allow for grear airflow, we can use 
        this to plan a cleaner city: 
        taller buildings should be built in the centre giving space to allow for airflow; 
        using shorter buildings to fill out spaces; 
        laying out the buildings in an even grid to reduce still air areas demenstrated by figure 3 type 3. 
        However at low density of buildings an uneven layout produces a more effective vortex, ("The strength 
        of the airflow inside the uneven street canyon models was obviously greater than that of the airflow 
        inside the even model under the steady inflow condition" Weijun Li. et al, (2020).).
